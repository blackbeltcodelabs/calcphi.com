How Defined Benefit Pensions Work

A defined benefit (DB) pension guarantees a specific monthly income in retirement regardless of investment performance. Unlike 401(k)s where your balance depends on market returns, your pension benefit is calculated by a formula — typically based on years of service, final average salary, and a benefit multiplier set by your employer.

Pension Benefit Formula

Monthly Benefit = Years of Service × Benefit Multiplier × Final Average Salary / 12

Most public sector pensions use a 1.5%–2.5% multiplier. Example: 25 years of service × 2% × $80,000 final salary / 12 = $3,333/month for life. This is equivalent to a $1,000,000 annuity purchased at retirement.

Pension vs 401(k) — Which is Better?

A pension with a 2% multiplier and 25 years of service at $80,000 salary pays $40,000/year for life — equivalent to a $1,000,000 portfolio at the 4% rule. This is why government and union pension jobs are so financially valuable. The risk: if your employer goes bankrupt, the Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation (PBGC) guarantees up to $7,053/month (2026) for single-life plans.

Tax Efficiency Across Accounts

Where you hold investments matters as much as what you hold. High-growth assets belong in Roth accounts where growth is tax-free. Income-producing assets like bonds belong in traditional 401(k) or IRA where taxes are deferred. Tax-managed index funds belong in taxable brokerage where you can harvest losses. This asset location strategy adds 0.2-0.4% annually to after-tax returns without changing your investments at all.

The lifetime value of proper tax planning for a median American household is approximately $150,000-300,000 in additional wealth at retirement — the difference between tax-smart and tax-naive investment management over 30 years. Most of this benefit comes from three decisions made once: choosing the right account types, maximizing employer match, and selecting low-cost index funds.

For retirement and tax calculations specifically, consider running this calculation once per year as your income, tax brackets, and contribution limits change. The IRS adjusts dozens of thresholds annually for inflation — limits that applied in 2023 differ meaningfully from 2026 figures. Bookmark this page and revisit each January after the new limits are announced.

Finally, remember that financial optimization is a long game. Improving your savings rate by 5%, reducing investment fees by 0.5%, and claiming every eligible deduction compound over decades into very large differences in final wealth. Small improvements made consistently outperform dramatic one-time decisions every time.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is my pension calculated?+
Most defined benefit pensions use: Years of Service × Benefit Multiplier × Final Average Salary. A 2% multiplier with 25 years at $80,000 final salary pays $40,000/year ($3,333/month) for life. Ask your HR department for your specific plan multiplier and how final salary is defined.
What is the difference between a pension and 401k?+
A pension (defined benefit) guarantees a specific monthly income for life regardless of markets. A 401(k) (defined contribution) depends on your contributions and investment returns — no guarantee. Pensions shift investment risk to the employer. 401(k)s shift risk to the employee.
Can I take my pension as a lump sum instead of monthly?+
Many plans offer a lump sum option. Compare by calculating the breakeven age — divide lump sum by monthly payment to get months to break even. If lump sum is $500,000 and monthly is $2,500, breakeven is 200 months (16.7 years). If you expect to live past breakeven, monthly is usually better.
