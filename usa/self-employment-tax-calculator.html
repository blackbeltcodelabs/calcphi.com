What is Self-Employment Tax and How Does It Work?

Self-employment tax (SE tax) is the freelancer and small business owner's version of FICA — Social Security and Medicare taxes. Employees have these split with their employer (each pays 7.65%). Self-employed individuals pay both halves: 12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare = 15.3% total, applied to 92.35% of net self-employment income.

SE tax applies only to the first $168,600 of net SE income for Social Security (2026 wage base). Medicare tax applies to all income, with an additional 0.9% surtax above $200,000 for single filers.

The 1/2 SE Tax Deduction — The One Tax Break Built In

The IRS allows self-employed individuals to deduct half of their SE tax from gross income when calculating AGI. On $80,000 net SE income with $11,303 SE tax, the deduction is $5,652 — reducing your taxable income by that amount. This partially offsets the employer-portion of FICA that traditional employees never see.

How to Reduce SE Tax Legally

  • S-Corp election — split income between salary (subject to SE tax) and distributions (not subject to SE tax). Often saves $5,000–$20,000+ on incomes above $80,000.
  • SEP-IRA or Solo 401(k) — contribute up to 25% of net SE income (up to $70,000 in 2026). Dramatically reduces taxable income.
  • Home office deduction — deduct a portion of rent/mortgage, utilities, and internet.
  • Health insurance premiums — 100% deductible for self-employed individuals.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I have to pay self-employment tax?+
You must pay SE tax if your net self-employment income is $400 or more in a year. This includes freelance income, independent contractor income, side business income, and gig economy earnings (Uber, DoorDash, Etsy, etc.). Report on Schedule SE attached to Form 1040.
How is self-employment tax different from income tax?+
SE tax (15.3%) covers Social Security and Medicare — separate from income tax. You pay both. On $80,000 net SE income, SE tax is ~$11,300 AND you also owe income tax on the net income. Total effective rate for most self-employed people is 25–35% of net income.
Can I deduct business expenses before calculating SE tax?+
Yes. SE tax applies to net profit — gross revenue minus allowable business expenses. Deductible expenses include home office, software, equipment, travel, professional fees, advertising, and more. Proper expense tracking can significantly reduce your SE tax bill.
