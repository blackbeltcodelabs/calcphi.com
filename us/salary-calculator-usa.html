State Income Tax — The Wildcard

State income tax varies dramatically across the US. Nine states have no income tax: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire (on wages), South Dakota, Tennessee (on wages), Texas, Washington, and Wyoming. California has the highest top rate at 13.3%. This makes state choice a significant financial factor — at $200,000 salary, living in Texas vs California saves approximately $20,000/year in state tax. How to Increase Your Take-Home Pay

  • Maximize pre-tax 401(k) contributions — reduces federal and state taxable income
  • Open HSA if enrolled in high-deductible health plan — triple tax advantage
  • Contribute to dependent care FSA if you have childcare expenses
  • Ensure correct W-4 allowances — over-withholding is an interest-free loan to the government
  • Consider increasing 401(k) to reduce taxable income into lower bracket

Frequently Asked Questions

How much of my salary goes to taxes?+
For most Americans earning $50,000–$150,000, expect to pay 25–35% of gross income in total taxes (federal + FICA + state). A $75,000 salary in a 5% state tax state results in roughly $52,000–$56,000 take-home, depending on filing status and pre-tax deductions.
What is FICA tax?+
FICA stands for Federal Insurance Contributions Act. It includes 6.2% Social Security tax (on wages up to $168,600 in 2026) and 1.45% Medicare tax (on all wages). Total FICA for employees is 7.65%. Employers pay a matching 7.65% on top of that.
How do I calculate my hourly rate from annual salary?+
Divide annual salary by 2,080 (52 weeks × 40 hours). $85,000 ÷ 2,080 = $40.87/hour gross. Take-home hourly (after all taxes): $85,000 take-home ÷ 2,080 ≈ $28/hour. This helps compare job offers with different structures.
